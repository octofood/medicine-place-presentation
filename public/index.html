The Problem

Canada's Generic Drug Crisis

Generics fill three-quarters of Canadian prescriptions. But Canada manufactures almost none of them.

0%
of prescriptions are generic drugs
In Plain Terms
Three out of four prescriptions filled at Canadian pharmacies are generics — amoxicillin for infections, metformin for diabetes, ramipril for blood pressure, sertraline for depression. Brand-name equivalents cost 5–10× more and often aren't covered by provincial drug plans. When a generic goes into shortage, patients face real treatment gaps with no affordable fallback.
Who's Tracking This
The CGPA compiles national dispensing data. CIHI tracks prescription volumes. Health Canada's Drug Shortage Database is the national reporting system. The Pharmaceutical & Life Sciences Sector Task Force (announced March 18, 2026) has 90 days to recommend structural solutions.
Sources & Links
CGPA annual reports · Health Canada, Building Resilience in Canada's Drug Supply 2024–2028

of generics sold in Canada are made here
In Plain Terms
88% of Canada's generic medicines are manufactured abroad — primarily India, China, and the US. Fewer than 10% of active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) are produced on Canadian soil. The entire oral generic supply chain, from raw ingredient to finished tablet, is overwhelmingly foreign-controlled. A single factory closure in Hyderabad or Wuhan can empty pharmacy shelves in Edmonton or Halifax.
Who's Investigating
EY Canada and the CGPA conducted a joint Manufacturing Capacity Study quantifying the gap. The CGPA and CAPDM published A Path Forward (2025) recommending domestic supply diversification. The Sector Task Force is now charged with acting on these findings.

In Plain Terms
The majority of drug shortages aren't caused by demand spikes or natural disasters. They originate from manufacturing failures — GMP compliance violations, production line contamination, raw material shortfalls, facility shutdowns — at factories overwhelmingly in India, China, and Southeast Asia that Health Canada cannot inspect, regulate, or influence. Canada has no line of sight into these facilities until shelves are already empty.

The Problem

The Black Box: What Happens Today

When an API supplier in India halts production of amoxicillin — Canada's most prescribed antibiotic — this is what follows.

Week 1
Nobody in Ottawa knows. Health Canada has zero visibility into upstream supply chains.
In Plain Terms
There is currently no system connecting Health Canada to upstream API suppliers. When a factory in Gujarat or Wuhan halts amoxicillin API production, no alert fires in Ottawa. Health Canada has acknowledged publicly: "the exchange of information about product demand and inventories continues to be a challenge." The disruption is invisible until pharmacy shelves are bare.
The Work Underway
Health Canada's 2024–2028 Shortage Plan, Priority 6 (Information Sharing), is meant to address this. New regulations in the Canada Gazette (Dec 28, 2024) require earlier reporting. But reporting a shortage after it starts is not the same as preventing one. The structural fix is upstream visibility — which requires either foreign cooperation or domestic production.

Week 3
Pharmacists notice. Wholesaler backorders appear. A mother in Edmonton drives to three pharmacies. None have children's amoxicillin.
In Plain Terms
Pharmacists — not federal regulators — are typically the first to discover a shortage, when wholesaler orders come back unfilled. The CPhA reports 1 in 4 Canadians have been directly affected. Clinically, this means parents driving to three pharmacies for children's amoxicillin suspension, psychiatric patients facing abrupt SSRI or lithium discontinuation (which carries seizure and relapse risk), and hospital pharmacists rationing injectable anesthetics.
Who's Tracking Patient Impact
The CPhA conducts annual surveys of frontline pharmacists. The Drug Shortage Database logs reports but does not capture patient-level clinical impact. There is currently no national system that tracks health outcomes resulting from drug shortages — a gap that multiple organizations have flagged.

Week 5
Provinces scramble. Emergency imports at 300-400% premiums. Alberta spends $2.1M on product that normally costs $600K.
In Plain Terms
When a critical generic runs out, provinces have no pre-negotiated backup. Emergency imports arrive at 300–400% markups — Alberta spent $2.1M on product that normally costs $600K. This cost falls directly on provincial health budgets already running deficits. There is no standing domestic supply contract, no federal stockpile mechanism for oral generics, and no price ceiling on emergency procurement.
What Needs to Exist
A pre-negotiated procurement instrument — such as a Contingent Standing Offer or NESS stockpile replenishment contract — would eliminate the emergency scramble. Provinces could call up domestic supply at pre-agreed prices instead of paying whatever importers demand. This is the gap the Sector Task Force has the mandate to address.
Week 14
The shortage "resolves." Emergency imports arrive. Prices normalize. Until the next one.
In Plain Terms
The shortage "resolves" when emergency imports arrive — but nothing structural changes. KPMG's Generics 2030 analysis shows this is a repeating cycle driven by market economics: prices collapse, manufacturers exit, supply concentrates, fragility increases. 12 Tier 1 medicines have been in continuous shortage for over 5 years. Each "resolution" is just a pause before the next failure.
The Structural Problem
KPMG was commissioned by the CGPA to analyze the generic market's long-term trajectory. Their conclusion: market forces alone will not fix this. The economics structurally drive consolidation and fragility. Government intervention — procurement mechanisms, stockpiling, domestic capacity support — is the only path to breaking the cycle. Every G7 nation except Canada has reached this conclusion and acted.

The Problem

The Structural Spiral: Why Shortages Keep Happening

This is not bad luck. KPMG's "Generics 2030" analysis reveals a structural market failure that guarantees recurring shortages.

Step 1
Demand Rises
Clinical Reality
Canada's population is aging, chronic disease prevalence is rising, and patent expiries are converting more branded drugs to generics annually. CIHI data shows prescription volumes increasing 3–4% per year. More patients on more generics means greater dependency on the same fragile supply chains — concentrated in a handful of overseas factories.
➜
Step 2
Supply Expands
The Mechanism
Multiple manufacturers enter the market to compete for growing volumes. Capacity expands — initially a healthy sign. But the economics of generic manufacturing (low margins, high regulatory cost, commodity pricing) mean this expansion is inherently unstable. It sets up the price collapse that follows.
➜
Step 3
Prices Fall
The Mechanism
Group purchasing organizations (GPOs) and provincial formularies use competitive bidding to drive prices down aggressively. Generic drug prices can fall 80–90% from original branded levels. In Canada, the PMPRB and provincial pricing policies compound this downward pressure. Good for consumers in the short term — but it hollows out the manufacturing base.
➜
Step 4
Margins Collapse
The Mechanism
At a certain price floor, manufacturers cannot sustain GMP-compliant production. Maintaining cGMP facilities, quality systems, stability testing, and regulatory filings costs money regardless of unit price. When margins approach zero, companies defer maintenance, consolidate lines, or stop investing in resilience. Quality risk rises as economic pressure mounts.
➜
Step 5
Manufacturers Exit
The Mechanism
Companies discontinue the least profitable molecules and exit smaller markets like Canada. Production lines close or consolidate. The Drug Shortage Database shows a steady stream of discontinuation notices — the new 12-month notice requirement (up from 6) was introduced precisely because manufacturer exits were accelerating. Fewer suppliers remain for each critical molecule.
➜
Step 6
Concentration & Fragility
The Mechanism
When two or three factories serve an entire country's need for a critical molecule, a single FDA Form 483 inspection failure, a single fire, a single quality hold creates a national shortage. This is Canada's current reality for many CVDL molecules — the supply base has concentrated to the point where single-point-of-failure risk is the norm, not the exception.
↻
Repeat
Shortage
The Bottom Line
The cycle repeats and each iteration leaves the system more fragile. This is why shortages are worsening despite government attention — the market structure itself produces fragility. KPMG, the CGPA, the CPhA, and Health Canada have all independently arrived at the same conclusion: market forces alone will not fix this. Deliberate policy intervention — procurement mechanisms, stockpiling, domestic capacity — is required to break the spiral.

What Government Has Built

Health Canada's 2024-2028 Shortage Plan

Eight priorities for building resilience — a blueprint that assumes capabilities the current market does not provide.

1

Critical & Vulnerable Drug List

Focus the system on the drugs where shortages cause the most harm

In Plain Terms
The CVDL is a federal evidence-based tool identifying 163 active ingredients where shortages cause the greatest clinical harm — antibiotics, cardiac drugs, anesthetics, psychiatric medications, chemotherapy agents, and more. It is not a wish list; it is an analytical product scored on clinical impact, supply vulnerability, and substitutability.
The Job That Needs Doing
The CVDL identifies which drugs matter most. It does not mandate how they are secured. No regulatory requirement yet links the CVDL to domestic production. The Sector Task Force is the mechanism that could connect the list to an operational procurement or stockpiling pathway.
Sources & Links
Health Canada, Critical and Vulnerable Drug List (final list ~April 2, 2026)
2

Shortage Prevention & Mitigation Plans

Require every CVDL drug holder to have an actionable plan and safety stock

In Plain Terms
Every company selling a CVDL drug must file a formal plan detailing how it will prevent and manage shortages — including supply chain risk assessments, contingency measures, and communication protocols. For importers, this means new compliance burdens on supply chains they don't fully control. A domestic manufacturer built from the ground up around CVDL compliance has a structural advantage.
Regulatory Timeline
The SPMP requirement was published in the Canada Gazette, Part II (December 28, 2024). Health Canada is currently consulting on implementation guidance. The first SPMPs are expected to be filed in 2026–2027.
Sources & Links
Canada Gazette, Dec 28, 2024 · Health Canada consultation on SPMP regulatory amendments
3

Strengthen Responses & Reporting

Better regulatory tools and broader reporting obligations

In Plain Terms
Health Canada is gaining sharper regulatory teeth: companies must now report demand surges exceeding 250% within 5 business days, provide 12-month discontinuation notice (up from 6), and comply with broader shortage reporting obligations. These are real enforcement tools — but they work after problems emerge. They improve response time without addressing root cause: that the production capacity is located offshore.
Sources & Links
Canada Gazette regulatory amendments · Health Canada, Building Resilience 2024–2028, Priority 3
4

Improve Reporting Systems

A single, modern national reporting platform (planned 2026-2027)

In Plain Terms
Health Canada is building a new national shortage reporting platform to replace the current fragmented system (the Drug Shortage Database relies on voluntary and mandatory company filings, which often arrive late). Target launch: 2026–2027. The limitation: even a perfect reporting platform can only report what the system can see. Without domestic manufacturing or upstream supply chain access, the data still arrives after shortages have already reached patients.
5

Build Analytics Capacity

Use better data to anticipate shortages before they happen

In Plain Terms
Priority 5 aims to predict shortages before they reach patients, using data analytics on supply signals, demand trends, and risk indicators. The problem: analytics require data inputs, and Health Canada currently has near-zero visibility into upstream API supply chains. Predictive models cannot work without production-level telemetry from manufacturing facilities — exactly the kind of data a domestic manufacturer could provide natively.
Sources & Links
Health Canada, Building Resilience 2024–2028, Priority 5
6

Improve Information Sharing

Confidential demand and inventory data shared across the system

In Plain Terms
Priority 6 calls for confidential data sharing across manufacturers, wholesalers, and provinces — demand forecasts, inventory levels, production schedules. This is the priority where the least progress has been made. Existing manufacturers treat supply chain data as commercially sensitive and are reluctant to share. The structural problem: asking multinational importers to provide transparency into supply chains they themselves don't fully control. A domestic manufacturer with built-in data sharing eliminates this impasse entirely.
7

Transparency & Communication

Better public and clinician information during shortages

In Plain Terms
Prescribers and dispensers need advance warning of shortages so they can identify therapeutic alternatives before patients arrive at the counter or the bedside. Currently, many clinicians learn about shortages only when a prescription cannot be filled. Better clinician-facing transparency tools would allow proactive therapeutic switching — but the data must exist upstream first.
8

Modernize Regulatory Frameworks

Ongoing regulatory evolution to improve access

In Plain Terms
Health Canada is shifting from reactive (managing shortages after they hit) to proactive (preventing them structurally). Key pending instrument: the Ministerial Reliance Order (expected spring 2026), which would allow Health Canada to rely on FDA and other trusted regulatory decisions to accelerate Abbreviated New Drug Submissions (ANDS). For a domestic generic manufacturer, this could cut years off the approval timeline for molecules already approved in the US or EU.
Sources & Links
Health Canada, Building Resilience 2024–2028, Priority 8 · Ministerial Reliance Order consultation (spring 2026)

New Regulations: The Compliance Pull

Canada Gazette, December 28, 2024 — new regulatory obligations that create structural demand for domestic manufacturing.

0
DINs requiring safety stock in Canada
In Plain Terms
A DIN (Drug Identification Number) is a unique identifier for each specific drug product — one molecule can have multiple DINs across strengths and dosage forms. 425 DINs covering ~70 CVDL molecules will require companies to maintain physical safety stock on Canadian soil. For importers, this creates new warehousing and working-capital costs they've never borne. For a domestic manufacturer, inventory is already here by definition.
Regulatory Detail
Published in Canada Gazette, Part II (December 28, 2024). Health Canada has released a Draft Guide to Safety Stocks for Drugs Containing Critical and Vulnerable Active Ingredients for consultation. The regulations define "in Canada" as physically located on Canadian soil — not in transit, not in a bonded warehouse abroad.

A domestic manufacturer produces fresh stock continuously and holds it on-site, meeting this requirement as a natural byproduct of operations rather than an added compliance burden.

Who Does What in Ottawa

Pharmaceutical resilience is not a single-department issue. Six federal actors each hold a piece of the puzzle.

🏥

Health Canada

Shortage policy, CVDL prioritization, regulatory pathway, safety stock policy, data visibility

Role & Authority
Health Canada is the anchor institution: publishes the CVDL, designs shortage regulations, manages the Drug Shortage Database, sets SPMP and safety stock policy, and chairs the Sector Task Force.
Limits of Authority
Health Canada cannot mandate a national off-take agreement, force provinces to buy from a specific manufacturer, or design procurement instruments. Its authority is regulatory and policy-setting — the purchasing authority sits with PSPC and provinces respectively.
🛡

PHAC / NESS

Stockpiling, emergency readiness, federally held pharmaceutical reserve

Role & Authority
PHAC manages the National Emergency Strategic Stockpile (NESS) — a federally owned reserve of medical assets including pharmaceuticals. NESS is explicitly federal jurisdiction (not provincial), making it the strongest institutional anchor for a domestic stockpiling argument. NESS replenishment contracts represent a direct federal demand signal.
Strategic Importance
NESS has been under-leveraged in previous pharmaceutical resilience proposals. NESS replenishment procurement for CVDL oral generics — a federal purchase, not a provincial one — could provide the demand certainty needed to anchor domestic manufacturing without requiring provincial buy-in as a prerequisite.
📜

PSPC

Procurement instrument design, standing offers, supply arrangements

Role & Authority
PSPC (Public Services and Procurement Canada) designs federal procurement instruments — standing offers, supply arrangements, and contract structures. If a CSO or similar mechanism is created, PSPC designs the structure, terms, activation criteria, and pricing framework. They are the "how" to Health Canada's "what" and PHAC's "why." PSPC has existing templates for medical supply standing offers from pandemic procurement.
🏆

DND

Defence readiness, medical countermeasures, Life Sciences Fund

Role & Authority
DND's 2026 Defence Industrial Strategy references medical countermeasures, pharmaceutical stockpiling, and domestic production resilience. The $656.9M Life Sciences Fund is a potential funding stream — applicable where molecules have genuine medical-readiness relevance (antibiotics for deployment, antidotes, emergency countermeasures). Ciprofloxacin (anthrax prophylaxis) and acetylcysteine (antidote) have clear defence connections.
Scope Discipline
Defence supports the case where the connection is genuine and documentable. Health-system resilience is the lead rationale. Overstretching the defence framing weakens credibility.
⚙

ISED

Industrial capability, innovation, regional development

Role & Authority
ISED frames pharmaceutical manufacturing as industrial capability and innovation. The Strategic Innovation Fund ($7.5B, 33 pharma projects funded to date) and PrairiesCan regional development programs could support facility development. ISED provides the economic development and job creation rationale that complements Health Canada's health-system rationale.
🇧🇨

Provinces

Formulary decisions, actual drug purchasing, patient care delivery

Role & Authority
Provinces are the actual drug purchasers and formulary managers — this is constitutionally provincial territory. Any federal mechanism must respect this jurisdiction. Federal instruments can create procurement pathways, standing offers, and stockpile mechanisms, but cannot force provinces to buy from specific manufacturers. Provincial buy-in requires demonstrated value: competitive pricing, reliable supply, and transparent quality data.
Coordination Principle
Health Canada as shortage/regulatory anchor. PHAC/NESS as federal stockpiling anchor. PSPC as procurement instrument designer. ISED and DND as supporting partners where legitimate fit exists. Provinces as purchasers with voluntary buy-in through demonstrated value. This multi-department coordination is exactly what the Sector Task Force is positioned to facilitate.
